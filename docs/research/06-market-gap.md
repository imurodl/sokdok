# 06 — Market gap & learner pain

> Research question: What do Korean learners actually struggle with re: reading speed, what do they use now, is there an unmet need, and is there money in it?

## 1. Real learner pain (TOPIK 3–5)

The complaint is near-universal: intermediate learners read slowly despite knowing the vocab and grammar.

- **Word-by-word translation bottleneck** — "I read one character by one character"; learners mentally translate each word to L1 instead of reading for meaning.
- **Context gaps** — too many unknown words per sentence break flow, force constant lookups.
- **No scaffolding between levels** — the jump from graded readers to native content (webtoons, news, novels) is a cliff. Graded readers feel artificial/boring; native webtoons have convoluted plots, slang, ambiguity → "0% comprehension" reports.
- **TOPIK time pressure** — Reading section is 70 min / 50 questions (TOPIK II); slow reading directly causes failure.

Real quotes (HiNative):
- "I can read Korean, but my speed is not good enough… What should I do to read faster?"
- "I read one character by one character. How do I increase my reading speed?"
- "I can read Hangul but only very slowly."

Sources: <https://hinative.com/questions/26547006>, <https://www.90daykorean.com/how-to-read-korean-faster/>, <https://koreanpractice.com/how-to-read-korean-faster/>

## 2. Current workarounds (community consensus)

- **Extensive reading of easy material** — children's books, webtoons of dramas you've seen; "just read a lot"; re-read passages.
- **Structural techniques** — stop translating word-by-word, accept ambiguity; finger-tracking to pace; mark unknown words but don't look up until end; read aloud repeatedly; pair slow comprehension-read with a timed speed-read of the same passage.
- **Materials** — webtoons (Naver/RIDI), celeb Instagram captions, Easy Korean News, K-pop lyrics, drama subtitles.

**None of this is built into a dedicated app** — learners stitch together Papago OCR + Naver Webtoon + dictionaries + video platforms manually.

## 3. The gap (clear, unmet)

The market is **bifurcated**:

- **Graded readers** = structure/leveling but inauthentic/boring → abandoned.
- **Native content** = engaging but zero scaffolding/leveling → overwhelming.

**No app bridges it.** Competitor matrix:

| App | Speed focus? | Leveling? | Native content? | Scaffolding? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| LingQ | No | user-driven | yes (import) | flashcards; weak for KO |
| Beelinguapp | limited | No | somewhat | context flashcards |
| Satori Reader (JP) | No | yes (6) | moderate | strong |
| Du Chinese (ZH) | No | yes (6) | yes | excellent |
| TTMIK | No | yes | No (educational) | strong grammar; boring |
| Lingopie | No | No | yes (K-drama) | minimal |
| Rakuten Viki | No | No | yes | tap defs; no leveling |

**Unmet need = a platform that:**
1. Grades/tags content by TOPIK/CEFR so learners know what matches their level.
2. Integrated vocab + grammar support in context (not separate flashcards).
3. **Explicitly measures reading speed (WPM) + comprehension %.**
4. Smooth progression graded → semi-authentic → native.
5. Keeps engagement with content people actually want (real webtoons/stories).

Forum evidence: learners literally ask "how do I know which webtoon to start with?" / "how to avoid too much slang?" — consensus answer is "trial and error." (<https://forums.learnnatively.com/t/graded-readers-to-webtoons/11811>)

## 4. Competitor success lessons

- **Du Chinese** — 4.9★ / 43,000+ reviews doing *just* graded reading. Wins on abundant graded native-level content + visible tracking ("words read") + daily 10–15 min habit. (<https://languavibe.com/du-chinese-review/>)
- **Satori Reader (JP)** — proves demand for the intermediate textbook→native gap; loved for in-context grammar ("like your own tutor") + audio + SRS. Risk: positioning identity crisis. (<https://languavibe.com/satori-reader-review/>)
- **Beelinguapp** — 4.7★; bilingual + audio reduces friction, but "consumption not learning" (no production); technical bugs frustrate. (<https://languavibe.com/beelinguapp-review/>)
- **LingQ** — flexible/massive library, but weak Korean leveling, no script instruction, overwhelming SRS. Readers want **structure and curation**, not infinite content. (<https://www.alllanguageresources.com/lingq-review/>)

**Common pattern:** authentic content + smart scaffolding + consistency tracking wins.

## 5. Market size & willingness to pay

- Korean language learning: **$7.2B (2024) → $67B by 2034** (CAGR 25.1%); mobile apps segment **$12.1B by 2034**; TOPIK/exam segment **$23.1B by 2034**. (<https://www.gminsights.com/industry-analysis/korean-language-learning-market>)
- Demand drivers: K-culture pipeline, TOPIK as career requirement (Samsung/Hyundai/LG), academic integration.
- Pricing: reading-specific tools sustain **$9–15/mo** (Du Chinese ~$9/$89yr at 4.9★; Satori ~$9/$89yr; Beelinguapp $6.99/mo).
- WTP higher for: reading-specific tools, TOPIK prep, habit/consistency tracking. Price sensitivity high for general learners (free TTMIK/Billy Go often out-rated).

## Conclusion

**A Korean reading-fluency app is a clear, actionable unmet need — not a crowded space.** Pain is real and specific; no direct competitor measures reading speed or bridges the graded→native cliff; adjacent markets (Du Chinese, Satori) prove demand and WTP. First-mover opportunity with a defensible content moat (difficulty-tagged native content) and Korean-specific scaffolding.
