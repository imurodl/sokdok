# 01 — The cognitive science of slow L2 Korean reading

> Research question: Why do intermediate Korean learners (TOPIK 3–5) read slowly even when they know most of the words?

## Headline finding

At TOPIK 3–5 the bottleneck is **not decoding**. Hangul is phonologically *shallow / transparent* — grapheme→phoneme mapping is regular, so an intermediate learner can already sound out almost anything. The slowness lives in three higher layers, all downstream of **automaticity** and **cumulative exposure**:

1. **Sight-vocabulary automaticity** — recognizing frequent words instantly vs. effortfully.
2. **Chunking** — processing multi-word units (collocations, particle+noun clusters) as single units.
3. **The word-by-word mental-translation habit** — translating each word into L1 instead of reading directly for meaning.

## Q1 — Why intermediate L2 readers are slow

- **Syllable-block (모아쓰기) coding.** Hangul packs jamo into square syllable blocks; readers assign letter positions *within* a block. Research shows a "very high degree of rigidity in orthographic coding" in Hangul. (Well-established for native children; the specific L2-intermediate burden is under-measured.)
  - <https://benjamins.com/catalog/wll.00027.pae>
  - <https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9010524/>
- **Sub-lexical → lexical route shift.** Beginners read letter-by-letter (sub-lexical); with exposure they shift to whole-word (lexical) recognition. Markers: word-length effects shrink, frequency effects grow. Documented in native Korean children; no direct L2-Korean study found.
- **Orthographic depth paradox.** Hangul is shallow for *decoding* but morphophonemically opaque for *spelling*. Because decoding is easy, intermediate slowness **cannot** be a grapheme-phoneme problem — it must be automaticity of sight vocabulary and chunking.
  - <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hangul_orthography>
- **Automaticity requires exposure hours.** LaBerge & Samuels (1974): fluent reading needs *automatic* word recognition, which frees working memory for comprehension. No source quantifies the exact hours needed for Korean at TOPIK 3–5.

## Q2 — Eye-tracking on Korean reading

- **L2 readers consistently show:** longer fixations, more first-pass fixations, longer gaze/dwell time, more total fixations, lower skipping rates, larger word-frequency effects, and weaker predictive processing than natives.
  - <https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9496816/> (Korean case processing, native vs. L2)
  - <https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7102/14/4/375> (scoping review of L2 eye movements)
- **Word spacing (띄어쓰기).** A 2021 Korean study found removing spaces *within* certain compound nouns actually **sped up** processing (automatic chunking of compounds) without hurting comprehension.
  - <https://accesson.kr/cogpsych/v.33/2/107/17781>
- **Gap:** no eye-tracking study maps native-Korean vs. L2-intermediate readers on fixations/saccades per syllable block. Optimal viewing position within Hangul blocks is unstudied.

## Q3 — Fluency vs. comprehension, and benchmarks

- **Fluency ≠ comprehension.** Fluency = speed + accuracy of *decoding* (WPM / words-correct-per-minute). Comprehension = understanding meaning. Fluency is the *bridge*: effortful decoding starves comprehension of resources.
  - <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10497962/>
- **Benchmarks (reference):** skilled adult natives ~250–300 WPM (English); competent L2 with controlled vocab 200–250 WPM; struggling L2 <100–150 WPM.
  - <https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0346251X22000835>
- **Native Korean reading speed ≈ 174 WPM** (ages 20–35, optimal acuity conditions). Cross-language WPM isn't directly comparable due to information density — track your *own* trend.
  - Korean WPM benchmark: <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10978959/>
- **Gap:** no validated per-TOPIK-level Korean WPM/글자수 benchmark exists in English-language literature.

## Q4 — What distinguishes fast from slow L2 readers

- **Sight-vocabulary automaticity** (fast, unconscious recognition of high-frequency words). Repeated-reading gains *transfer* to new text → true automaticity, not memorization.
- **Chunking ability** — the strongest single predictor of reading efficiency; intermediates chunk far less than natives.
  - <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7844092/>
- **Naming speed (RAN)** predicts fluency across all languages (double-deficit hypothesis).
- **"Fast but shallow" caveat:** faster L2 reading can reflect shallow parsing — always measure comprehension alongside speed.
- **Subvocalization** is *load-bearing*, not waste — it is part of word recognition, not something to eliminate.

## Q5 — The theory

- **LaBerge & Samuels (1974), Automatic Information Processing.** Practice → automatic word recognition → attention freed for meaning. This is the direct theoretical basis for **repeated reading** (Samuels' own intervention).
  - <https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/0010028574900152>
- **Perfetti — Verbal Efficiency Theory / Lexical Quality Hypothesis.** Higher-quality lexical representations (orthographic + phonological + semantic) = faster, more efficient recognition. Validated as a predictor of L2 processing efficiency (better than proficiency tests).
  - <https://www.researchgate.net/publication/286433386_The_lexical_quality_hypothesis>
  - <https://link.springer.com/article/10.3758/s13421-023-01436-0>

The two theories are complementary: automaticity = *effort reduction*; lexical quality = *representation quality*. Both require repeated exposure to words in context.

## Well-established (high confidence)

- Automaticity theory applies to L2; repeated reading builds automaticity and transfers to new text.
- L2 readers fixate longer/more, skip less, show bigger frequency effects, predict less.
- Chunking ability predicts L2 reading efficiency; naming speed predicts fluency universally.
- Hangul is transparent for decoding but opaque for spelling.
- Lexical quality (Perfetti) predicts L2 processing efficiency.

## Design implications for Sokdok

- Don't fight decoding — build **sight-vocab automaticity** and **chunk recognition**.
- Kill the **word-by-word translation** habit (gloss on tap, not inline translation; encourage reading-for-gist).
- Measure **WPM + comprehension** together so "fast but shallow" is caught.
- The lever is **engaged volume**, delivered as level-appropriate, measured, repeated reading.
