# 03 — Reading-speed methods, ranked by evidence

> Research question: What methods actually improve L2 reading speed/fluency, and how strong is the evidence?

## Summary ranking

| Rank | Method | Verdict | Gains | Best for |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Extensive Reading (ER)** | STRONG ✓ | *d* = 0.83–0.98 for speed; 200k words/yr threshold | All levels; sustainable |
| 2 | **Timed Repeated Reading (TRR)** | STRONG ✓ | +52% in 9 weeks (141→214 WPM) | Building automaticity on texts |
| 3 | **Reading-While-Listening (RWL)** | MOD–STRONG ✓ | Modest speed, large fluency/listening | Lower proficiency; listening lag |
| 4 | **Graded readers** | MODERATE ✓ | Subsumed in ER | Scaffolding ER (i+1) |
| 5 | **Sight-word automaticity drills** | MODERATE ✓ | Small–moderate; foundational | High-frequency vocab baseline |
| 6 | **Chunking / eye-span / anti-subvocalization** | DEBUNKED ✗ | None (physiological limits) | Not recommended |
| 7 | **RSVP / Spritz** | DEBUNKED ✗ | Speed up, comprehension down | Not recommended |

## 1. Extensive Reading — STRONG ✓

Large-volume reading of comprehensible, level-appropriate material builds automaticity via cumulative exposure.

- Between-group effect sizes for **reading speed**: *d* = 0.83–0.98 (higher than comprehension gains at 0.54–0.63).
- **Beglar & Hunt (2014): ~200,000 words/year is the threshold** for observable fluency gains; below it, minimal improvement.
- Longitudinal (2024): 8+ pages/week → ~33% speed reduction over 26 weeks; largest gains for the *slowest* baseline readers.
- Caveat: speed gains alone can mask shallow parsing — monitor comprehension.
- Sources: <https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7188915/>, <https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/education/articles/10.3389/feduc.2024.1286132/full>

## 2. Timed Repeated Reading — STRONG ✓

Read the same passage multiple times against a clock; automaticity on familiar text transfers to new text.

- **Chung & Nation (2006): 52% speed improvement (141 → 214 WPM) over 9 weeks — tested on Korean learners.**
- Combined ER + TRR + repeated oral reading beat ER alone (+51.55 vs +38.57 WPM, Shimono).
- Best on level-appropriate (i+1) texts.
- Sources: <https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1316861.pdf>, <https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0346251X22000835>
- Paul Nation's "Reading for Speed and Fluency" series targets 250 WPM with controlled vocabulary.

## 3. Reading-While-Listening — MODERATE–STRONG ✓

Simultaneous read + synced audio (karaoke highlight). Audio scaffolds the weaker skill.

- 26-week RWL study: >100% dictation-score improvement; strong vocab gains.
- **Most effective when listening lags reading** (typical for L2). No detrimental effects observed.
- Orthographic distance L1↔L2 modulates benefit (Koh 2024).
- Sources: <https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/flan.12732>

## 4. Graded readers — MODERATE ✓

The delivery vehicle for ER. Effective for reading rate; works best paired with genuine interest ("reading gain without reading pain"). TRR on graded texts enhances gains.

## 5. Sight-word automaticity drills — MODERATE ✓

- **Automaticity ≠ knowledge** — *knowing* a word vs. *accessing it instantly* are different constructs; the second predicts fluency.
- ~95% word-family coverage needed for independent reading; ~2000 families for basic expression.
- Necessary foundation, **not sufficient alone**. Larger gains when words are embedded in sentence/discourse context, not isolated.
- Sources: <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC11333505/>, <https://academic.oup.com/applij/advance-article/doi/10.1093/applin/amaf042/8179356>

## 6. "Speed reading" techniques — DEBUNKED ✗

- **Subvocalization** is necessary (part of word recognition), not waste. Eliminating it is a myth (Scott Young 2015 retraction).
- **Perceptual span** is a hard physiological limit (~3–4 letters left, 14–15 right of fixation); not dramatically trainable.
- **Chunking ceiling:** ~1 word/fixation (exceptional ~2.5); ~4 fixations/sec → hard ceiling ~600 WPM. 1000+ WPM claims contradict eye-tracking.
- **Regressions are beneficial** — preventing them *decreases* comprehension (Schotter et al. 2014).
- When speed-reading training works (Klimovich 2023), the mechanism is **metacognition** (goal-awareness, strategy), not eye mechanics.
- Sources: <https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1529100615623267> (Rayner et al. 2016), <https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1467-9817.12417>

## 7. RSVP / Spritz — DEBUNKED ✗

Words flashed one at a time to eliminate saccades.

- **Impairs comprehension**, especially on longer/denser text.
- Eliminates parafoveal preview and reader control of fixation duration; prevents beneficial regressions.
- Spritz specifically: "impairs literal comprehension and increases visual fatigue."
- Sources: <https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1529100615623267>, <https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0747563214007663>

## Key takeaways for Sokdok

1. Speed development is **slow, cumulative, input-driven** — the 200k words/year anchor. No shortcut bypasses volume.
2. **Combination beats single method**: ER + TRR + RWL + automaticity drills work synergistically.
3. **Speed ≠ depth** — always measure comprehension with speed.
4. **Do not build** RSVP/Spritz or eye-span/anti-subvocalization features — evidence says they hurt.
5. **Metacognition matters** — a visible goal + a timer is itself part of why timed reading works. Sokdok's WPM chart and TRR timer are not gimmicks; they are the mechanism.
